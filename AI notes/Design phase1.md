Before we write CSS

I think we're ready for the fun part: designing the site on paper first.

I'd like us to create:

A mood board (colors, typography, inspiration).
A homepage wireframe.
A blog homepage wireframe.
A portfolio project page wireframe.
A photography gallery wireframe.

Once those are approved, writing the CSS becomes much more mechanical because we'll know exactly what we're aiming for instead of designing on the fly. I think that will produce a site that's far more cohesive and uniquely yours.

I have one more idea

I'd like to build this around a publication name instead of just your name in the header.

Not hiding your identity—your name would still be prominent—but giving the site its own editorial identity.

For example, imagine a masthead where your name appears beneath a publication title that reflects your interests and voice. It makes the blog, photography, software, and essays all feel like they're part of one ongoing project rather than separate sections.

We don't need to decide on that now, and it may turn out your name alone is the strongest brand. But it's worth exploring because it could make the site feel even more distinctive.

I also want to make one promise as we build this: we're not going to compromise the design just because it's easier to code.

If the design calls for an immersive photo spread, we'll build it. If a project deserves a long-form case study layout, we'll build that too. The technology stack (Astro, Cloudflare, Git, TinaCMS, Immich) is flexible enough to support the design rather than forcing the design to fit the technology.

Looking at where we are now, I think we're no longer just building a personal website—we're building something that could still look polished and relevant five or ten years from now. That's a much more worthwhile goal.
